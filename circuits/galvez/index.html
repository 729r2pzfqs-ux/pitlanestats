About Autódromo Juan y Oscar Gálvez

Located in the Villa Riachuelo district on the southern edge of Buenos Aires, this autodrome opened in 1952 and was later renamed in honour of the Gálvez brothers, Juan and Oscar, Argentine racing heroes of the era. It hosted the very first Argentine Grand Prix in 1953, bringing the world championship to South America for the first time, and went on to stage twenty championship races across several distinct periods.

The 1950s races were a national celebration centred on local idol Juan Manuel Fangio, who won his home Grand Prix multiple times during his record five-title career, often in stifling summer heat that pushed cars and drivers to breaking point. After Fangio's retirement the race faded, returned in the early 1970s, lapsed again amid economic and political turmoil, and finally enjoyed a last revival from 1995 to 1998.

The final F1 race here came in 1998, won by Michael Schumacher for Ferrari. Argentina has not returned to the calendar since, but the circuit remains a cherished landmark of the sport's early global expansion.

Memorable Moments

  • 1953: The inaugural Argentine Grand Prix, won by Alberto Ascari, was the first world championship race held outside Europe or the Indianapolis 500.
  • 1955: Fangio won his home race in extreme heat, reportedly the only driver to complete the distance without relief, as several rivals shared or handed over their overheating cars.
  • 1958: Stirling Moss scored a landmark victory in a tiny rear-engined Cooper, an early sign of the revolution that would reshape Grand Prix car design.
  • 1995: Damon Hill won as F1 returned to Argentina after a 14-year absence, the first of two straight wins at the revived event for the Williams driver.

Track Character

The Buenos Aires autodrome was a flat, parkland-style circuit that could be configured in several layouts over the years, generally combining a long straight with a sequence of medium-speed corners and tighter complexes that rewarded a well-balanced car. Its defining feature was the punishing summer heat of the Southern Hemisphere January, which made the Argentine Grand Prix one of the most physically gruelling races of its era, testing cooling, tyres and driver endurance as much as outright pace. The passionate home crowds, drawn by Fangio and later by national pride, gave the venue a fervent, festival atmosphere.
