About Circuit de Monaco

The Circuit de Monaco is the most famous race in the world, a Grand Prix run through the streets of Monte Carlo since the very first Formula 1 World Championship season in 1950. Threading past the harbour, the Casino, the tunnel and the celebrated swimming pool complex, it is the jewel in the crown of motorsport — one third, alongside the Indianapolis 500 and Le Mans 24 Hours, of the unofficial Triple Crown.

Almost unchanged in its essential character for decades, Monaco is a circuit where the margins are measured in centimetres. The barriers loom inches from the cars, there is virtually no run-off, and the slightest error ends a driver's afternoon against the Armco. It has long been the ultimate showcase of car control, where a great qualifying lap can mean as much as a great race.

The race carries unmatched glamour and prestige, drawing royalty, celebrities and superyachts to the principality each May. For all the debate about its lack of overtaking in the modern era of large cars, victory at Monaco remains one of the most coveted prizes any driver can win.

Memorable Moments

  • 1982: A chaotic final few laps saw a string of leaders fall away — Alain Prost crashed, Riccardo Patrese spun and recovered — and Patrese ultimately won amid one of the most bizarre finishes in F1 history.
  • 1984: In torrential rain, a young Ayrton Senna was closing rapidly on leader Alain Prost in his uncompetitive Toleman when the race was stopped early, denying him a stunning maiden win and announcing his genius.
  • 1988: Senna, by now utterly dominant, built a huge lead before crashing out of the race at Portier — a rare error that he later described as a deeply formative moment in his career.
  • 1992: Senna held off a charging Nigel Mansell, whose Williams was far faster, over the closing laps in a masterclass of defensive driving on a track where passing is almost impossible.

Track Character

Monaco is the slowest and tightest circuit in Formula 1, a relentless sequence of barriers, elevation changes and blind corners that leaves no room for error. From the uphill climb to Casino Square, down through Mirabeau and the Fairmont hairpin — the slowest corner in F1 — into the tunnel and out to the harbour chicane and the swimming pool, it demands total commitment and pinpoint precision. Overtaking is famously near-impossible, which makes qualifying paramount: pole position is often decisive, and a single perfect lap, where drivers brush the barriers at the exit of every corner, can define the entire weekend. Ayrton Senna mastered it like no other, taking a record six wins, while Graham Hill earned the nickname "Mr Monaco" for his five victories in the 1960s.
