The 1997 Season

The 1997 season produced one of the most dramatic and controversial title finales in Formula One history, with Jacques Villeneuve winning the championship for Williams-Renault after a season-long battle with Michael Schumacher's Ferrari. Villeneuve, in his second season, drove the strong Williams FW19 to seven victories, while Schumacher, continuing Ferrari's resurgence, pushed the championship to the wire despite the German team still not having quite the fastest package. The two men entered the final round at Jerez separated by a single point in Schumacher's favour.

The defining moment came at that European Grand Prix at Jerez, where Schumacher led and Villeneuve attempted a bold passing move into a right-hand corner. As Villeneuve dived down the inside, Schumacher turned his Ferrari into the Williams in an apparent attempt to take both cars out of the race, a manoeuvre reminiscent of his 1994 Adelaide collision. This time, however, it backfired spectacularly: Schumacher's Ferrari beached itself in the gravel while Villeneuve, though damaged, continued and finished third, securing enough points to be crowned World Champion.

The FIA took an unprecedented step in response, disqualifying Schumacher from the entire 1997 World Championship for what it deemed a deliberate attempt to collide with a title rival, stripping him of his runner-up position in the standings, though it allowed him to keep his individual race results. It was a remarkable sanction against the sport's biggest star and a clear signal that the governing body would no longer tolerate the kind of premeditated contact that had marred earlier title deciders. The verdict shaped Schumacher's reputation for years to come.

Beyond the Jerez drama, 1997 was notable for the rising competitiveness of McLaren-Mercedes, who returned to winning ways after a barren period and signalled the partnership that would dominate the seasons ahead. Williams took the constructors' championship, but it would prove the team's last for many years, as the loss of Renault's works engine support after the season would blunt its competitiveness. The year thus marked both the peak and the beginning of the decline of the great Williams-Renault era, and confirmed that the next great rivalry would pit Schumacher's Ferrari against the silver cars of McLaren.
