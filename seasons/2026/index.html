The 2026 Season

The 2026 season opened a brand-new chapter for Formula 1, ushered in by the biggest regulation overhaul in a generation. Sweeping new rules brought a fresh generation of power units with a far greater share of electrical power and fully sustainable fuels, alongside active aerodynamics and cars that were shorter, narrower, lighter and more nimble. As with any major reset, the new regulations reshuffled the competitive order, and the early rounds revealed a dramatically different pecking order from the McLaren-dominated season that preceded it.

Mercedes seized the new era emphatically. George Russell won the season-opening race to lay down an early marker, but it was rookie sensation Andrea Kimi Antonelli who stole the show, reeling off a stunning streak of five consecutive victories through the opening rounds to surge to the top of the championship as an unexpected early title favourite. His run of dominance, built on pole positions and commanding race pace, established Mercedes as the team that had best understood the new rules and turned the young Italian into the breakout story of the campaign.

Antonelli's streak was finally broken at Round 7, where Lewis Hamilton delivered a milestone victory for Ferrari, his first win for the Scuderia and a timely sign that the established order was beginning to respond to Mercedes' fast start. At Round 8 Mercedes struck back on Red Bull's home ground: George Russell converted pole into a commanding win at the Red Bull Ring, with Max Verstappen second and Antonelli recovering from his Barcelona retirement to complete the podium and extend his championship lead. With the season still in its early stages and the new machinery rapidly evolving, the championship picture remains wide open, but the story so far is one of a bold technical reset rewarding Mercedes and announcing Antonelli as a genuine contender for the sport's biggest prize.

Round 9 brought a sprint weekend at Silverstone, and a new twist in the title fight. Antonelli converted Saturday's sprint into his first career sprint victory, but Sunday belonged to Ferrari: Charles Leclerc ended a winless run stretching back to 2024, taking the British Grand Prix under a late safety car ahead of George Russell and Lewis Hamilton after Max Verstappen spun out of contention at Stowe with four laps remaining. A five-second track-limits penalty, triggered by a wheel-shield failure, left Antonelli only 16th in the Grand Prix, trimming his championship advantage over Russell to 25 points as Ferrari consolidated second in the constructors' standings.
