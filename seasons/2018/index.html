The 2018 Season

The 2018 season was, in many ways, a continuation and intensification of the Hamilton-versus-Vettel battle that had defined the previous year. Ferrari arrived with arguably the fastest car on the grid for much of the season, and Sebastian Vettel started strongly, winning the opening round in Australia and trading the championship lead with Lewis Hamilton through the first half of the year. For a time it appeared Ferrari finally had both the machinery and the momentum to end Mercedes' run of dominance.

The turning point came through a familiar pattern of Ferrari and Vettel errors at critical moments. Vettel crashed out of the lead at his home German Grand Prix in wet conditions while Hamilton charged from fourteenth on the grid to win, a swing of enormous psychological and points significance. Further mistakes followed, including a collision with Hamilton at Monza and a spin in Japan, while Ferrari's strategic missteps and a controversial technical clampdown on its power unit blunted its second-half pace.

Hamilton, by contrast, was nearly flawless when it mattered, producing a series of imperious drives and capitalising on every Ferrari slip. His Mercedes team responded to early-season pressure by sharpening its operations, and Hamilton pulled away decisively in the autumn. He clinched his fifth world championship at the Mexican Grand Prix, moving past Juan Manuel Fangio's haul and into rarefied company with only Michael Schumacher ahead of him on the all-time list.

Mercedes secured both championships once more, extending its remarkable run of dominance in the hybrid era. The season reinforced the narrative of Ferrari and Vettel squandering competitive machinery through unforced errors and operational weaknesses, a theme that would haunt the team for years. It also featured the continued ascent of Max Verstappen, the retirement of Fernando Alonso from Formula One, and growing anticipation of a generational changing of the guard, even as Hamilton stood ever more firmly atop the sport.
