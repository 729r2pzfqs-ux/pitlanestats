About Eddie Irvine

Eddie Irvine was a driver from Northern Ireland, born on 10 November 1965 in Conlig, County Down, known for his sharp wit, brash confidence and uncompromising racecraft. He reached Formula 1 with Jordan in 1993, marking his debut at the Japanese Grand Prix with a points finish and an infamous clash with reigning champion Ayrton Senna that ended in a confrontation in the paddock.

His career was defined by his move to Ferrari in 1996, where he served alongside Michael Schumacher. After years as a loyal number two, his moment arrived in 1999: when Schumacher broke his leg at Silverstone, Irvine stepped up to lead Ferrari's title charge, winning four grands prix that season and taking the championship battle with Mika Hakkinen to the final round in Japan, where he finished runner-up by just two points. Across 147 starts he scored 4 wins, 26 podiums and 191 points. He left Ferrari for Jaguar in 2000, leading the new team until his retirement after the 2002 season.

Irvine is remembered as the man who came within a whisker of the 1999 World Championship and as one of the sport's most outspoken personalities, a driver who blended genuine pace with an unfiltered, larger-than-life character.

Racing Style & Legacy

Irvine was a tough, opportunistic racer who thrived when given a clear chance to lead. For most of his Ferrari years he played the supporting role to Michael Schumacher with discipline, but the 1999 season proved he had the speed and nerve to fight for the title himself. Combined with his famously blunt media presence, he remains one of the most memorable characters of late-1990s Formula 1.
