About Mercedes

Mercedes-Benz holds a unique place in Grand Prix history. The German marque dominated the very first years of organised motor racing, returned to win in 1954 and 1955 with the legendary W196 and Juan Manuel Fangio, then withdrew after the 1955 Le Mans disaster. It would be more than half a century before Mercedes returned as a full works team.

That comeback, completed when the squad took over the championship-winning Brawn operation for 2010, produced the most dominant period in the sport's history. From 2014 to 2020, powered by a masterful interpretation of the new hybrid power-unit regulations, Mercedes won seven consecutive drivers' and constructors' championships. Lewis Hamilton claimed six of those drivers' titles and Nico Rosberg took the seventh in 2016, while the Brackley team set records for wins and poles that may stand for generations.

Even after the regulation reset of 2022 ended the streak, Mercedes remained a frontline team and a benchmark for engineering depth, with George Russell leading a new generation alongside its long-serving technical leadership.

Key Achievements

  • Eight consecutive Constructors' Championships (2014–2021)
  • Drivers' titles for Hamilton (2014, 2015, 2017–2020) and Rosberg (2016)
  • Fangio's dominant World Championship-winning W196 campaigns of 1954 and 1955
  • Record-setting win and pole tallies during the hybrid era
  • One of the most successful works programmes in motorsport history

Legacy & Identity

The three-pointed star represents engineering precision and ruthless efficiency. Mercedes turned the complex hybrid era into a showcase of total dominance, combining works-team resources, a relentless culture of marginal gains and the talents of Lewis Hamilton into a juggernaut. Its legacy is the definition of a modern Formula 1 superpower.
